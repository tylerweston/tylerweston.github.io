Astrocytes: The New Star of Learning and Long-Term Memory

Tyler Weston

Abstract:

        Classical neuroscience studies viewed glial cells as mere scaffolding that the brain was built on. Research over the last two decades has pointed to a far more active role of astrocytes in many aspects of cognition. This paper will argue that astrocytes form an integral part of the learning process in both the developing and adult brain. Astrocytes are integral to plasticity in the brain and are the drivers behind the formation of new synaptic pathways, both in the developing and adult brain, and it is this plasticity that allows for the formation of new memories and the learning of new abilities. Astrocytes are able to both receive information from synapses through metabotropic and ionotropic channels, and modulate both pre- and postsynaptic strength. By interacting with nearby neurons, astrocytes are able to form efficient small world networks for optimal information processing. Astrocytes are able to signal other astrocytes on both a local and global level through calcium wave propagation. These calcium waves contain information that conveys both fast, on the scale of milliseconds, and slow, on the scale of seconds, modulations across a variety of distances and scales that provide the perfect mechanism for integrating complex and cross-modal memories that allow us to learn complex and abstract new information.  Through these varying signalling and information integration techniques, astrocytes are central to the formation of memories and learning in both developing and adult brains.

Astrocytes: The New Star of Learning and Long-Term Memory

Since pioneering work on plasticity by psychologist William James and neuroscientists like Santiago Ramón y Cajal, plasticity in the brain has long been postulated to be a key factor in learning and memory formation. The preeminent view of the brain at that time was called the neuron doctrine, that neurons were the sole functional unit responsible for cognition. The importance of neuron-glia interplay in the brain was first hypothesized by Carl Ludwig Schleich in his book Schmerzlose Operationen 1894. He believed astrocytes played a role in regulating levels of activation across synapses. In 1907 Ernesto Lugaro proposed that astrocytes influence activations at synaptic clefts by taking up and metabolizing neurotransmitters, a hypothesis finally confirmed through experimental means in 1994 (Hubbard & Binder, 2016), leading to the idea of the tripartite synapse, consisting of an astrocyte in contact with pre- and postsynaptic terminals and modulating activations across the synaptic cleft (Araque et al. 1999). It’s only been in the last two decades that astrocytes have begun to be viewed as potentially just as important as neurons with regards to the computational and information processing capabilities of the brain.    

Astrocytes are known to provide metabolic support for neurons, amongst other homeostatic processes (Hertz, Dringen, Schousboe, & Robinson, 1999; Stobart & Anderson, 2013; Tabata, 2015), and controlling the formation and elimination of synapses (Chung, Allen, & Eroglu, 2015). They also release the S100B protein, which, in proper amounts, is shown to be required for learning and memory, but when overexpressed can lead to memory dysfunction, for example, in Alzheimer’s disease (Kielbinski & Sołtys, 2009). While the only direct causal link between astrocytes and memory formation so far shown has been through metabolic support and S100B protein (Adamsky & Goshen, 2018), these are merely structural support roles for astrocytes. In this paper, I will argue for a functional role for astrocytes in learning and memory both during childhood development, and in adults via neurogenesis, modulation of brain wave oscillations, and plasticity.

There are many different theories on the different types of memory systems. We will consider primarily long-term memory, as opposed to working memory, and then within long-term memory a distinction between declarative memory, that is, facts, autobiographical details, and other knowledge that is consciously accessible and reportable, and procedural memory,  motor skills and abilities, things we know how to do but cannot necessarily describe.

        Learning is a lifelong process, starting in our childhood and hopefully continuing into seniority. Learning involves integrating multimodal sensory stimuli with existing memories to form new long term memories. We learn what is similar or different across linked events and attempt to distinguish meaningful differences between the situations (Komorowski et al., 2013). There are deep structural and functional links between memory and learning, both of them supporting each other in ways that make the systems difficult to separate (Wickelgren, 1981). For the sake of brevity, I will treat these systems as one integrated unit, only differentiating between the processes where necessary. Learning is the acquisition of skill or knowledge, usually backed by repetition in short-term memory, and then stored in long-term memory for later retrieval (Camina & Güell, 2017).  There are many different areas of the brain thought responsible for memory formation and retrieval, such as the amygdala via the integration of information (McGaugh et al., 1993), the basal ganglia (Graybiel, 2005) and the dorsal striatum in motor tasks (McDonald & White, 1993), and the hippocampus, thought to underlie much learning and memory (See for review Malenka & Nicoll, 1999; Nicoll, 2017). The hippocampus has long been considered an integral part of memory (Jarrad, 1993; Penfield & Milner, 1958; Scoville & Milner, 1957), through spatial memory (Morris, Anderson, Lynch, & Baudry, 1986), learning and remembering sequences and temporal order of events (Fortin, Agster, & Eichenbaum, 2002), and associative learning (Gould, Beylin, Tanapat, Reeves, & Shors, 1999; Mahut, Zola-Morgan, & Moss, 1982).  

        The origins of astrocytes in the developing brain is still not entirely well-understood in humans, since the ideas behind the birth and lineage of astrocytes are based on rodent studies. Since astrocytes seem to be present in many diverse species though (Herculano‐Houzel, 2014), it seems likely that similar processes take place in the human brain as in other mammal brains, probably to an even greater degree due to the increased complexity and proliferation of astrocytes (Oberheim, Wang, Goldman, & Nedergaard, 2006). During the early phases of neurogenesis in the developing brain, radial glial cells are derived from neuroepithelial cells from the walls of the neural tube. These radial glial cells participate in neurogenesis until just prior to birth, when they will begin producing astrocytes (Chandrasekaran, Avci, Leist, Kobolák, & Dinnyés, 2016). So after the main period of neurogenesis, gliogenesis occurs (Miller & Gauthier, 2007), and it is only after this period of astrocyte differentiation that synaptogenesis begins (Freeman, 2010), directed by astrocytes (Adamsky & Goshen, 2018; Sloan & Barres, 2014). Children up to three years of age actually overproduce connections between neurons, and it is only after this early period that infrequently used synapses begin to be pruned and connections used often are strengthened. Astrocytes play a key role in the control of Hebbian plasticity, the idea that “neurons that fire together wire together” (Fellin, 2009). This synaptogenesis is species specific and based on the contextual expectations of recurrent experiences (Hannon, 2003). Early childhood learning and the astrocytes that drive this synaptogenesis are vital for building a strong base for future learning.

Plasticity is a change in the underlying structure of the brain either through the addition or removal of connections, or an alteration of the connection strength of existing connections (Fu & Zuo, 2011). Plasticity is hypothesized to play a central role in memory formation (Abraham, Jones, & Glanzman, 2019; Martin, Grimwood, & Morris, 2000). During motor skill learning, reorganization of existing neural networks is observed in relevant areas, such as the primary motor cortex, cerebellum, and striatum (Ungerleider, Doyon, & Karni, 2002). Plasticity in the developing and early childhood brain is paramount to developing the memory and learning systems that will be used for the rest of the child’s life (Nelson, 2000).

        By disrupting glycogenolysis in astrocytes, young chicks had trouble both consolidating new experiences into long-term memory and recall during short-term memory use (Gibbs, Anderson, & Hertz, 2006; Gibbs, Hutchinson, & Hertz, 2008), showing the necessity of astrocytes to both learning new, novel concepts, and crystallizing that information for long term retrieval. Glycogenolysis is an astrocyte-specific process that is vital for the learning process and establishment of new memories. While this process incorporates different neurotransmitters such as serotonin and noradrenaline, the main signalling agent is glutamate, produced and maintained by astrocytes  (Hertz, Dringen, Schousboe, & Robinson, 1999; Hertz & Chen, 2018).    

        The question of neurogenesis in the adult brain is one that is only recently starting to be answered (Gonçalves, Schafer, & Gage, 2016; Spalding et al., 2013). One particularly interesting area is the dentate gyrus, which is linked with the formation of new episodic memories. The dentate gyrus is also one of the few areas where neurogenesis occurs in  the fully developed, adult brain, driven by signalling from astrocytes (Song, Stevens, & Gage, 2002; Sultan et al., 2015). These newly produced neurons are highly plastic and are believed to contribute to learning and memory (Gonçalves, Schafer, & Gage, 2016). Episodic memories are necessary for setting and following through on short-term goals, visualization, and autobiographical remembering (Conway, 2009). How episodic memories are extracted from external stimuli and crystallized into long-term memory is still unknown, but it seems like learning and placing stimuli in their current context is important. Research suggests a separation of object and event as being parsed in separate brain areas, which then converge on the hippocampus where they are bound into one memory, and linked with associated memories (Eichenbaum, Sauvage, Fortin, Komorowski, & Lipton, 2012).

What about procedural learning? In mice that have been selectively bred to have reduced Ca2+ signalling, they were found to be less able to learn new motor tasks, specifically forearm reaching task, as opposed to a control group. In addition to the importance of calcium signalling, mice whose astrocytes were metabolically inhibited through the use of fluorocitrate showed similar inabilities to learn new motor tasks. When these mice were then administered a gliotransmitter D-serine, which regulates neurotransmitters and synaptic plasticity, the mice’s learning rate improved even against the inhibited astrocytes. D-serine is a glutamate coagonist, so functions similar to the glutamates that would be produced by astrocytes during normal functioning. (Padmashri, Suresh, Boska, & Dunaevsky, 2015). We also see that learning motor skills increases both the number of synapses, a process driven by astrocytes, and the volume of astrocytes within the cerebellar cortex. It is important to note that rats that merely exercised, that were physically active just as much as the rats that were learning, did not experience the same increase in astrocyte volume (Anderson et al., 1994; Kleim et al., 2007), showing it is the function of learning rather than merely providing cerebrovascular support that leads to a change in astrocyte physiology.    

Changes in the electrophysiology of the brain have been shown to be important to both long-term memory formation and learning in humans, (Elazar & Adey, 1967; Klimesch, 1999), as we see increased coherence in theta waves during tasks such as encoding, retrieval, and extinction of context fear in mice (Corcoran, Frick, Radulovic, & Kay, 2016); rats learning new rules for navigating mazes (Benchenane et al., 2010); and changes in gamma wave oscillations in mice engaged in object recognition tasks (Lee et al., 2014). The last experiment here is particularly telling, as it separated neural effects from astrocyte effects on the gamma waves, showing that astrocytes are able to influence brain waves independent of neurons. Astrocytes play a key role in the modulation of brain waves and therefore large scale neuronal activity via modulation of extracellular potassium clearing rates (Bellot-Saez, Kékesi, Morley, & Buskila, 2017; Bellot-Saez et al., 2018).  

        Small world networks are local grouping of neurons that show optimization for speed, efficiency, power, and synchronizability (Watts & Strogatz, 1998). In computer models of small world neural networks, they are seen to be more efficient at storing and retrieving memories as well (Morelli, Abramson, & Kuperman, 2004). By taking information from nearby synapses through both ionotropic and metabotropic signalling channels (Agulhon et al., 2008), and then influencing the activities across synapses (Perea, Navarrete, & Araque, 2009), new models suggest viewing local groups of neuron and astrocyte ensembles as forming small world networks (Guo, Tang, Ma, & Wang, 2017; Sasaki, Kuga, Namiki, Matsuki, & Ikegaya, 2011). Studies in neurodegeneratives disease point to the importance of these small world networks in learning and retaining memories. In Alzheimer's patients, functional connectivity studies have shown a reduction in brain network structures that resemble small world networks (Stam, Jones, Nolte, Breakspear, & Scheltens, 2007), and one of the hallmark symptoms of Alzheimer’s is memory loss. Similar results are seen in patients with hepatic encephalopathy, which is thought to lead to neurological impairment relating to memory loss through premature senescence of astrocytes (Görg, Karababa, & Häussinger, 2018), which leads to disruptions in the small world structure of the brain as revealed through fMRI studies (Zhang et al., 2014). Memory loss in Alzheimer’s has also been linked to disruption in calcium signalling, the primary communication method of astrocytes. (Berridge, 2011)

Astrocytes are capable of communicating with each other through extracellular calcium signalling (Bezzi & Volterra, 2001; Cornell-Bell, Finkbeiner, Cooper, & Smith, 1990; ). This calcium signalling happens across a large variety of scales, both spatial and temporal (Santello, Toni, & Volterra, 2019). This variety of spatial scales is important as, opposed to the close-knit neighbors of neurons that communicate unidirectionally with each other, astrocytes could potentially operate on a mesoscale, incorporating distance brain areas into integrated brain states, while still allowing a fine-grained enough interaction with individual synapses and local groups of neurons (Santello, Toni, & Volterra, 2019). The different temporal scales that astrocytes are capable of responding to, as opposed to the fast responses of action potentials, can also allow for integrating larger scale, more abstract concepts in the brain across longer time scales (Fields et al., 2014). Calcium signalling has also been shown to be integral to synaptic plasticity (Lamont & Weber, 2012) which, as discussed above, is central to memory formation and learning. By integrating information from different areas of the brain and then directing plasticity  neurogenesis and synaptic plasticity, it seems rather likely that astrocytes play a key role in the learning and storing of complex memories.          

While there is much circumstantial evidence pointing to a functional role for astrocytes in memory and learning, this field of study is still in its infancy. Further research and experimental paradigms will be needed to begin to explore some of the unanswered questions. Some possible avenues for exploration are optogenetics (Figueiredo et al., 2011), which has, for example, shown that fMRI results utilizing blood oxygenated-level dependent signals that were believed to be exclusively neural phenomena can be generated by astrocytes with no neural activity present (Takata et al., 2018). Optogenetics may help us further differentiate between neural and astrocyte responsibilities when it comes to memory and learning and other cognitive tasks of the brain. Chemogenetics offers another possible vector of inquiry, as Adamsky et al have used stimulation of astrocytes in the hippocampus to improve memory while also showing that direct stimulation of the relevant neurons would lead to cognitive impairments (2018). While the results obtained through optogenetic experiments are impressive, future goals will be a refinement of the technique to allow closer replication of natural functions of astrocytes, both individually and at the population level, to allow a closer study of the natural behavior of astrocytes in the human brain (Adamsky & Goshen, 2018). An optimal experiment would be able to somehow separate entirely the functional effects of astrocytes on learning and memory from their well-established structural roles.

The view of astrocytes as mere support players, the glue that holds the brain together, is rightfully being left behind as the result of new experiments and results. A more complete view of astrocytes as being important in forming functional networks with groups of neurons is emerging and replacing the established neural doctrine. This new neural-glial view is offering explanations for questions about learning and memory while opening new avenues for exploration, and providing us a deeper understanding of how we learn and remember.
